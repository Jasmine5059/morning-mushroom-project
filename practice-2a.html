Practice 2

Symbiotic

Ghosts

Rhizome Taoist cosmology Becoming Posthuman ecology Posthuman art

In this installation, I used three projectors, an iPad running the Pepper's Ghost illusion, and a layered arrangement of artificial moss, sculpted clay mushrooms, coral forms, and yarn to make mushrooms and jellyfish resonate with one another across the same space. What drew me to this pairing was a feeling I kept returning to in my research: that fungi and jellyfish share a quality of formlessness, of existing at the edges of what we recognise as individual life. I wanted to see what happened when I placed them in the same field, letting the virtual and the real coexist without resolving into each other.

The philosophical concept of "becoming" proposed by Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari provide the theoretical inspiration for this work. The installation constructs a symbiotic environment where life forms coexist across a three-dimensional space comprising one horizontal surface (the ground) and two vertical surfaces. A central theme involves the dissolution of dimensional boundaries.

Name: Symbiotic Ghosts

Venue: Unfolding Narrative Exhibition 2025, (Upper Gallery, London College of Communication)

Equipment: Three projectors, iPad

Materials: Artificial moss, sculpted clay mushrooms and coral, yarn

Publication Status: A reflective article based on this practice was published in AnthWays, Goldsmiths, read here .

Folding Futures

The video content deliberately blurs the distinctions between terrestrial and marine ecosystems, showcasing the transformation and evolution of multiple species. I found that the AI-generated animations produced something I had not fully anticipated: it manifested what Deleuze terms the "repetition of difference," presenting "infinite variations of folding" (Hélène Frichot，2009), specifically where each frame constitutes a micro-fold of the previous one.

The Pepper’s Ghost illusion reflects the dynamic intersection and coexistence of terrestrial and marine organisms, specifically rotating spaces, swimming creatures, and growing plants inhabiting the same vibrant field. Within this intricately layered corner, the work balances intentional design with natural stochasticity to articulate themes of freedom and generative potential.

Through the integration of multi-species imagery, specifically lichens, fungi, and corals, with AI-generated visuals, spatial folding structures, and Pepper’s Ghost illusion technology, my work established a temporary ecosystem. This system emphasised relational existence and a state of continuous becoming, facilitating a perceptual overlap between terrestrial and marine environments, biology and technology, as well as matter and imagery.

This installation aims to generate a fungal "rhizome" and "a map" wherein audience movement, dwelling, and perceptual engagement inform the generative process, ensuring that ecological relationships are experienced as an unfolding phenomenon.

Working with this installation clarified for me what the subsequent stages of my doctoral research need to build toward. It validates the feasibility of integrating Deleuze-Guattarian thought into artistic practice as a generative mechanism, while demonstrating the capacity of installation art to function as a relational research site where non-human agency, perceptual becoming, and post-human ecological awareness manifest.
