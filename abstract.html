Practice-based Research / 01.Abstract

Abstract

ABSTRACT

We live in ‘postnormal times,’ a condition characterised by complexity, chaos, and contradictions (Sardar, 2020). In the aftermath of the COVID-19 pandemic, which exposed the fragility of human exceptionalism and the entanglement of human life with non-human biological processes, artificial intelligence has undergone rapid and largely ungoverned expansion, with capital flowing into AI systems at an unprecedented scale while social anxieties around unemployment, economic precarity, and the erosion of creative labour accumulate (Dyer-Witheford et al., 2019). The boundaries between human cognition, machinic processing, and ecological process are dissolving faster than existing frameworks can account for. In this context, the question of how humans, technologies, and non-human life might find new modes of collaboration, attunement, and care has become urgent in ways that extend well beyond academic debate.

This practice-based research project explores how interactive digital art might reshape perception within the rapidly evolving technological context of AI, through a body of work comprising interactive installation exhibitions and participatory workshops. Drawing on Daoist philosophy, Deleuzo-Guattarian thought, and posthuman knowledge, I investigate the possibilities of co-evolution between human bodies, non-human entities, and environments, asking how digital technologies might, like mycelia, generate new perceptual systems built on symbiosis, infiltration, and temporary connection.

The interactive exhibitions take the form of generative imagery, sensor-based interactive installations, and embodied participation, observing how perception is generated and modulated in the relations between body, algorithm, and technology. The participatory workshops guide participants into what I call a "symbiotic community" composed of somatic activity, creative making, and algorithmic processes.

Research Questions

1. How can Daoist philosophy, Deleuzo-Guattarian thought, and posthuman theory be brought together to provide a coherent theoretical foundation for my practice, and what productive tensions emerge from holding these bodies of knowledge in relation?

2. Within interactive installation and workshop contexts, how does AI-mediated interactive digital art shift perception toward fluid, embodied, and situated forms of experience, and how does participatory interaction activate new sensory states and generate relational modes of knowing?

3. How does the experimental space of shared ‘composting’ formed through interactive digital media (Haraway, 2016), grounded in principles of co-becoming, responsiveness, and generativity, function as a site of resistance to the large-scale human anxieties provoked by AI's rapid expansion?
