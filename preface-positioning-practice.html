Positioning Practice

Positioning Practice as the Cultivation of the Morning Mushroom

In my research, practice is understood as a field of continuous becoming, a cultivated, infected, and participatory mushroom bed where knowledge grows, differentiates, and diffuses. I situate myself within the practice, participating in the generative process alongside materials, technology, the environment, and the body.

Pelbart's research examines how Guattari's concept of the ecology of the virtual opens toward heterogeneous, ecopolitical processes that operate across mental, social, and environmental dimensions, offering a framework for understanding how artistic practice might participate in the production of new modes of existence. Drawing on this foundation, my practice resembles the construction of a 'virtual ecology' (Guattari, 2000; Pelbart, 2023). Through the direct participation of immersive imagery, interactive installations, generative artificial intelligence, and the body, I attempt to build an open and fluid space co-constituted by technology, imagery, others, human and non-human. Technology here represents an existence with materiality and perceptual agency. Somatic responses, emotional fluctuations, linguistic errors, and cultural translations all alter the forms and meanings of data.

Within my practice, the status of the image loosens from fixity as the generative system takes on its own productive agency. Generative art establishes a symbiotic ecology where human creativity and machine intelligence are intertwined, making algorithmic training, parameter fluctuations, and model feedback become integral parts of this ecology. This framework shifts the artist's role from manual creator to a strategist who manages an evolving, autonomous system. Ultimately, this ecology ensures that the artwork is not a static product but a process-oriented experience, where the interplay of human and machine keeps the art in a constant state of becoming.

The bodies of audiences and participants enter the field, becoming media that can be triggered and influenced. In my installations and workshops, rhythms of breath, pauses in pace, and changes in posture act as micro-disturbances absorbed and reallocated by the system. In this process, the audience is embedded into the generative network as temporary nodes. Ecology unfolds continuously through the intersection of multiple relationships, between body and algorithm, between stillness and movement, between the human and the non-human.

Through repeated cycles of growth, decay, and mutation, the practice itself becomes a research apparatus. Generative rules, interactive logics, perceptual pathways, and strategies of uncertainty are tested, refined, and translated across contexts. In this sense, practice actively advances research by producing situated knowledge. Ultimately, process-based methodology provides a path that can be accessed, perceived, and continuously grown for post-human, non-anthropocentric, and non-controlling ecological art practices.
