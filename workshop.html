Reimage the relationship between AI and human through Daoist Cosmology: Patterns of the Unseen in Co-creation

As human-AI collaboration becomes central to participatory design (PD), current approaches remain grounded in Western frameworks that treat AI as either an instrumental tool or an autonomous agent. However, this binary fails to address how AI can participate in culturally situated, embodied meaning-making processes. We explore how Daoist cosmology reclaims AI as an organic participant through Qi-based epistemologies. Using a series of participatory workshops that integrated Qi Gong movement practice, drawing, I Ching divination, and AI-generated images, we examined whether tacit correspondences could emerge between human expression and algorithmic output. Our findings reveal unexpected visual and affective alignments between participants' hand-drawn images and pre-generated AI outputs despite no information transfer. We identify mechanisms of "Tacit Resonance" where Qi practice expanded perceptual capacities, enabling recognition of patterns that crystallised meaning retroactively through encounter. We conclude with implications for participatory design that embrace relational cosmology and tacit attunement, arguing for methodologies grounded.

The series of collaborative workshops functions as a full-process trial of the practice model established in this research. Three participatory workshops integrated Daoist somatic practices, divination systems, and generative artificial intelligence to explore a non-instrumental mode of human-AI collaboration. Within each workshop, participants first achieved a perceptual state of the body and Qi through Tai Chi or Qigong exercises, subsequently engaging in abstract drawing to express immediate feelings regarding current life dilemmas. Following the completion of these drawings, the research team employed I Ching hexagram divination to pair each participant with a pre-generated AI image corresponding to the structures and symbolic meanings of the 64 hexagrams.

This research proposes a fundamental reorientation of participatory design: from extracting latent knowledge to cultivating perceptual readiness. Where dominant approaches treat participation as the articulation of pre-existing needs, our Daoist-informed framework reveals it as the expansion of what can be sensed, felt, and collectively rendered meaningful. We argue that tacit resonance emerges when design creates conditions for new ways of knowing to arise through embodied encounter. This positions AI within a relational ontology where agency is distributed, indeterminacy is generative, and meaning unfolds through mutual becoming.

We had prepared these images in advance by prompting the AI with language drawn from the symbolic structures of all 64 hexagrams. The prompt I used for Hexagram 43, Guai, for instance, read:

"A dramatic sky where light breaks through dark storm clouds like a sword of justice, inspired by I Ching Hexagram 43 (Guai), water rising toward heaven, symbolising decisive power and cosmic clarity, radiant golden light splitting the heavens, powerful Taoist symbolism, bold and dynamic composition, cinematic atmosphere, intense contrast."

The AI system had no contact with the content of the participants' drawings during the generation process. Nevertheless, most participants perceived unexpected visual similarities and emotional affinities upon comparing the two images, specifically in terms of compositional structure, rhythmic lines, natural imagery, and overall atmosphere. Through one-on-one reflective dialogues, these correspondences were described by participants as a form of "resonance" or "response." The workshops thus revealed a creative mechanism where meaning manifests progressively through attunement and recognition between somatic practices, symbolic systems, and computational processes instead of originating from unilateral human intent or algorithmic calculation.

These experiences gave me a practical understanding of what a human-AI collaboration method based on Qi and symbolic resonance might actually feel like from the inside. They are the ground from which the next phase of my research grows.

Daoist "Generative Logic" as Practical Experience

I regarded workshops as a generative research apparatus. Practice involving Qigong and divination prompts participants to imagine future possibilities, such as dwelling locations, vocations, and the unfolding of life. Within this framework, imaginative activity functions as a generative condition, allowing thought and perception to enter an open structure while activating new modes of association. Participants described moments of "resonance" wherein emotional and symbolic synchronicity was perceived during practice. This resonance became clarified through subsequent dialogue and reflection, eventually forming an integral part of experiential understanding.

The components of AI imagery and drawing guided a practical turn toward figures and symbols. Under the guidance of Qi, participants generated drawings, abstract forms, and symbolic images within an open context. Here, the "figure" exists as a relational entity, echoing the "figurations" proposed by Haraway (2016) and the post-human "cartographies" discussed by Braidotti (2018). Figures participate in the generative process and operate at the intersection of somatic movement, symbolic systems such as I Ching hexagrams, images generated by technical algorithms, and dialogic reflection. They enter relational networks and exert influence upon the generative process.

Implications of the Workshop as a Research Apparatus for Doctoral Research

On a methodological level, the workshop constitutes a research apparatus where somatic practices, symbolic systems, AI-generated images, environmental contexts, and interpersonal interactions form a holistic structure. This practice model provides a stable yet open framework for this doctoral research project. Consequently, the workshops serve as sites where these philosophical trajectories encounter one another, intertwine, and generate new insights. This provides methodological support for practice-based and post-qualitative research, enabling relational, post-human, and non-representational dynamics to unfold while promoting a creative understanding that is non-human-centric and non-Western-centric.
