Encountering Morning Mushroom

Encountering Morning Mushroom

We are taught to become Chun (the Toona tree椿).

To grow tall enough, long enough, stable enough.

To possess a main trunk, annual rings, a lineage, and a history.

To organise the world into forms

that can be inherited, preserved, and archived.

I stand with Zhaojun (the morning mushroom, 朝菌).

It knows nothing of dusk or dawn.

It does not preserve itself for the future.

It falls into the soil, spreads, borrows, disappears.

The Chun understands life as continuity.

The Zhaojun knows the world is already a network.

I refuse the "Toona-style" subject.

Choosing a downward method instead.

To let technology lose control.

To let systems leak.

朝 菌 不 知 晦 朔 蟪 蛄 不 知 春 秋 此 小 年 也 冥 灵 大 椿

Morning mushrooms know not

the transition of eve and dawn, and cicadas

know not the passing of spring and autumn;

this is the nature of the short-lived. In the south of Chu,

there is the Mingling tree, which regards five hundred years as a single spring

and five hundred years as a single autumn. In high antiquity, there was

the Great Toona tree, which regarded eight thousand years

as a single spring and a single...

a long stem of time

descending through scales

of life and duration

This section traces the personal and philosophical encounters that give rise to the central research questions of this study. It moves from the Daoist figure of the morning mushroom toward an emerging connection between psychedelic perception and generative artificial intelligence. These encounters collectively prompt a central enquiry: whether immersive and generative ecological art practices might open new perceptual pathways, offering alternative ways of sensing, feeling, and being in the world.

Zhuangzi described these two temporalities over two millennia ago. The morning mushroom emerges at dawn and vanishes by dusk, whereas the Great Toona tree grows infinitely upward with eight thousand years as its seasonal cycle. Time here collapses into a mismatch of scales. I am drawn to this misalignment: a life spanning only a single day, seeping downward to unfold within the damp, dark layer of decay where invisible mycelia connect, entwine, and thrive.

Taoist texts remind us that life is no longer merely an upward ascent. It involves a return, a descent, and a blending. Mushrooms differ from the organised expansion of the Great Toona tree as they cling, branch, and disappear only to reappear suddenly elsewhere. They prioritise entering and becoming the other over maintaining their own fixed form. Fungi grow amidst ruins, stitching the world together at the ruptures of capitalism. The metaphors of the morning mushroom and the Great Toona tree function as a methodology for this research, providing a way to think through entanglement, becoming, and alterity.

This same loosening, I would later find, has a technological counterpart. When discussing generative AI visuals, viewers often instinctively associate them with altered perceptual states. Images oscillate between stability and collapse, as seen in uncanny temporal distortions visible in AI video generation platforms. Structures lose control and re-crystallise while figures constantly deform, flee, and re-territorialise, resembling the visual logic of consuming certain toxic fungi.

Both mushrooms and AI large language models were, in a sense, cultivated in the dark: fungi grow beneath the soil in conditions invisible to us, while AI systems emerge from vast, opaque datasets within what is commonly called the black box. Both carry within them something toxic and something sweet. Mushrooms can poison or nourish. AI likewise operates as a system of toxicity and drops of sweetness simultaneously, generating outputs that can disorient, manipulate, or open unexpected ways of sensing and thinking.
